What Happened When a City Removed All Its Parking Meters

Currentwave · 9 March 2026

When Ghent removed parking meters from its city centre and replaced street parking with bike lanes and café terraces, critics predicted economic collapse. Three years later, retail revenue in the affected zones is up 18 percent.

The logic was counterintuitive but simple: cars passing through don't spend money. People who walk, cycle, and linger do. A parking space serves one person. A bench serves dozens. The economics of public space favour humans over vehicles, but it took decades of car-centric planning to obscure that fact.

The transition wasn't painless. Delivery logistics had to be rethought. Residents with mobility needs required dedicated drop-off points. But the city treated these as engineering problems, not reasons to abandon the project. Solutions emerged because they had to.

Other European cities are watching closely. The question is no longer whether car-free zones work, but how quickly the rest of the world will catch on.
