The Library That Bends With the Wind

Atelier · 6 March 2026

The new public library in Stavanger, Norway, is designed to move. Its timber-lattice roof flexes up to eight centimetres in high winds, absorbing the force rather than resisting it. The engineers took their cue from Viking longships, which survived North Sea storms not through rigidity but through controlled flex.

Inside, the movement is imperceptible. But on stormy days, a subtle creak runs through the building — the sound of architecture doing exactly what it was designed to do. The patrons barely notice. The building is working so they don't have to worry.

The structure uses cross-laminated timber throughout, making it one of the largest public buildings in Scandinavia to achieve near-zero embodied carbon. The wood was sourced from managed forests within 200 kilometres of the site, and when the building eventually reaches end of life, every component can be disassembled and reused.

It's a philosophy that applies beyond engineering. The best structures — physical, organisational, personal — are the ones that bend without breaking.
