The Concrete House That Feels Like a Forest

Atelier · 21 March 2026

From the street, it's a modest concrete box on a wooded hillside in southern Chile. Step inside and the walls dissolve. Floor-to-ceiling glass wraps three sides, framing the native beech forest so completely that the architecture disappears.

The architect, María Paz Undurraga, describes the design as "a frame for what was already there." The concrete is board-formed, bearing the grain of the timber moulds, so even the solid walls carry the texture of the forest. Heating comes from a single wood-burning stove at the centre of the open plan.

No tree was removed during construction. The foundation sits on steel piles driven between root systems, and the cantilevered deck hovers above the forest floor without touching it. The building treads so lightly on the site that moss has already begun growing underneath it.

The house cost less to build than a conventional home of the same size. The restraint isn't a budget compromise — it's the entire point.
