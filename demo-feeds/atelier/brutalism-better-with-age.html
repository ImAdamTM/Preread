Why Brutalism Looks Better With Age

Atelier · 12 March 2026

Brutalist buildings were controversial when they were new. Half a century later, the ones that survived demolition campaigns are among the most photographed structures in their cities. The Barbican in London. Habitat 67 in Montreal. The National Theatre on the South Bank.

Concrete ages in a way that glass and steel don't. It stains, weathers, and accumulates character. Moss fills the board-formed grain. Rain streaks create patterns that the architect never drew. The building becomes a collaboration between the designer and time itself.

The current generation of architects citing Brutalist influences aren't nostalgic; they're practical. Exposed concrete is honest, durable, and requires almost no maintenance. In an era obsessed with sustainability, a building material that lasts a century without a paint job deserves a second look.

Perhaps the lesson is that beauty in architecture, as in people, is sometimes a function of time rather than intention.
