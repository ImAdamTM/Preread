The Deepest Hole Ever Drilled (and Why We Stopped)

Prism · 17 March 2026

In 1970, Soviet scientists began drilling into the Kola Peninsula with a simple goal: go as deep as possible. Over the next 24 years, they reached 12,262 metres — a record that still stands. Then they stopped, not because they ran out of ambition, but because the Earth fought back.

At that depth, temperatures hit 180 degrees Celsius, far hotter than models predicted. The rock behaved less like a solid and more like a plastic, closing the borehole behind the drill bit. The project revealed that our understanding of what lies beneath the crust was, in many ways, guesswork.

Among the surprises: liquid water was found at depths where none was expected to exist. Microscopic fossils of plankton appeared in rock two billion years old. The boundary between granite and basalt, predicted at five kilometres, never materialised at all.

We've sent probes to the edge of the solar system, but the deepest we've ever gone into our own planet is about 0.2 percent of the way to the centre. The Earth keeps most of its secrets close.
