Why the Night Sky Is Disappearing

Prism · 14 March 2026

A third of humanity can no longer see the Milky Way. Light pollution is increasing at roughly 10 percent per year, driven by LED lighting that is cheap, efficient, and pointed in every direction except useful ones.

The ecological consequences are now well-documented. Migratory birds lose their bearings. Sea turtle hatchlings crawl toward streetlights instead of the ocean. Insects, which navigate by moonlight, spiral into bulbs until they die of exhaustion.

But there's a human cost too. For most of history, every person who looked up at night saw the same staggering canopy of stars. That shared experience is quietly vanishing, replaced by an orange glow that tells us nothing about where we are in the universe.

The solutions are surprisingly simple: shielded fixtures that point light downward, warmer colour temperatures that scatter less in the atmosphere, and motion sensors that turn lights off when nobody needs them. The technology to fix this problem already exists. What's missing is the will to use it.
