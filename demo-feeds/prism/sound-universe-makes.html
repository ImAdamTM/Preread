The Sound the Universe Makes

Prism · 2 March 2026

Space isn't silent. NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory has been translating astronomical data into sound since 2020, and the results are haunting. Galaxy clusters hum in B-flat, 57 octaves below middle C. Black holes groan at frequencies so low they'd take 10 million years to complete a single oscillation.

The technique is called sonification — mapping data that we normally visualise into audio frequencies we can hear. It's not artistic licence; it's a genuine analytical tool that reveals patterns invisible on a graph. Astronomers studying the Bullet Cluster found that sonification made the separation between dark matter and normal matter audible as a distinct tonal shift — something the visual data hadn't made obvious.

The universe, it turns out, is full of pressure waves — the same phenomenon we call sound on Earth, just propagating through plasma instead of air. In galaxy clusters, where hot gas fills the space between stars, sound waves ripple outward from violent events like supermassive black hole jets. The waves are real. We just can't hear them without translating them up by a factor of several hundred trillion.

There's something unsettling and beautiful about hearing the cosmos. The recordings sound like nothing on Earth — deep, resonant drones that seem to come from everywhere and nowhere. They remind you that the universe is not just something to look at. It's something happening, constantly, at scales we can barely conceive.
