What Octopuses Dream About

Prism · 19 March 2026

When an octopus sleeps, its skin ripples through a kaleidoscope of colours and textures. Researchers at the University of Washington believe these displays correspond to dream states, much like the rapid eye movements in sleeping mammals.

The colours shift in patterns that mirror waking behaviour: hunting sequences, camouflage responses, even what appears to be social signalling. If the octopus is dreaming, it may be replaying its day in chromatophore.

A brain like no other

What makes this remarkable is that octopus brains are organised nothing like ours. Two-thirds of their neurons — roughly 350 million of them — are in their arms, not their heads. Each arm can taste, touch, and make decisions semi-independently. An octopus reaching into a crevice for food is, in a sense, sending an autonomous agent to do the job while the central brain attends to other matters.

The central brain itself is a donut shape wrapped around the oesophagus, which means every meal the octopus swallows passes directly through its brain. Evolution, evidently, does not share our preference for tidy engineering.

Despite this alien architecture, octopuses demonstrate behaviours we associate with sophisticated cognition: tool use, observational learning, play, and now, apparently, dreaming. They've been observed unscrewing jars from the inside, navigating mazes after a single attempt, and squirting water at lab assistants they don't like — repeatedly, and only at specific individuals.

Two roads to consciousness

If octopuses dream, they've arrived at the same cognitive trick through an entirely different evolutionary path. Mammals and cephalopods diverged over 500 million years ago — before backbones, before jaws, before anything we'd recognise as a brain. Whatever drives dreaming, it was either invented twice independently or was present in an ancestor so ancient it predates most animal body plans.

Either possibility is staggering. Convergent evolution of dreaming would suggest that any sufficiently complex nervous system eventually develops the need to replay its experiences during rest — that dreaming isn't a mammalian quirk but a fundamental feature of neural computation. And if it's ancestral, it means the roots of consciousness run deeper than we've ever imagined.

The researchers are cautious about using the word "dream." What they observe are "active sleep episodes" characterised by rapid colour changes, body twitching, and altered brain activity. Whether the octopus experiences anything subjective during these episodes — whether it "sees" images, "feels" sensations — remains unknown.

The hard question

The question it raises is as old as philosophy: what does it feel like to be an octopus? Thomas Nagel famously asked the same question about bats in 1974, arguing that consciousness is inherently subjective and may be impossible to access from the outside. If that's true for a fellow mammal, it's doubly true for a creature whose body and brain share almost nothing with our own.

We may never know. But watching one dream — its skin flickering through colours we can see but it can't, its arms curling around phantom prey — is as close as we're likely to get. And perhaps that's enough. Not to understand what it feels like to be an octopus, but to understand that it feels like something.
