What Octopuses Dream About

Prism · 19 March 2026

When an octopus sleeps, its skin ripples through a kaleidoscope of colours and textures. Researchers at the University of Washington believe these displays correspond to dream states, much like the rapid eye movements in sleeping mammals.

The colours shift in patterns that mirror waking behaviour: hunting sequences, camouflage responses, even what appears to be social signalling. If the octopus is dreaming, it may be replaying its day in chromatophore.

What makes this remarkable is that octopus brains are organised nothing like ours. Two-thirds of their neurons are in their arms, not their heads. If they dream, they've arrived at the same cognitive trick through an entirely different architecture, suggesting that sleep and dreaming may be more fundamental to intelligence than we assumed.

The question it raises is as old as philosophy: what does it feel like to be an octopus? We may never know. But watching one dream, its skin flickering through colours we can see but it can't, is as close as we're likely to get.
