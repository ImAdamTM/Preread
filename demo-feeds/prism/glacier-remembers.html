The Glacier That Remembers Everything

Prism · 11 March 2026

Ice cores drilled from glaciers in Greenland and Antarctica contain air bubbles trapped hundreds of thousands of years ago. Each bubble is a tiny time capsule, preserving the exact composition of the atmosphere on the day the snow fell.

By reading these bubbles layer by layer, scientists have reconstructed 800,000 years of climate history with startling precision. They can identify individual volcanic eruptions, trace the rise of agriculture through methane spikes, and watch carbon dioxide levels climb and fall with ice ages.

The process of extracting this information is painstaking. A single ice core can take years to analyse. Each layer must be dated, its gases measured with instruments sensitive enough to detect parts per billion. The researchers work in cold rooms at minus twenty degrees, handling samples with the care of archivists preserving ancient manuscripts.

The current CO2 concentration — around 425 parts per million — is higher than anything in that 800,000-year record. The ice doesn't editorialise. It just reports. But the data speaks clearly enough.
