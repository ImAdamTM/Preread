The Hidden Cost of Worrying About Getting Older

By Jamie Ducharme
Published on March 27, 2026
Fact checked by
Nick Blackmer
Fact checked by Nick Blackmer
Nick Blackmer is a librarian, fact-checker, and researcher with more than 20 years of experience in consumer-facing health and wellness content.

  • A new study suggests that anxiety about getting older may be linked to faster biological aging.
  • Researchers found that worries about aging—especially health concerns—were associated with signs of accelerated aging.
  • Instead of trying to eliminate those worries, experts say it may be more helpful to focus on managing anxiety.

Worrying about getting older won’t make you any younger—in fact, a recent study suggests, it might do the opposite.

Women who feel anxious about aging may actually age faster, biologically speaking, than those who are calmer, according to the study, which was published in the journal Psychoneuroendocrinology.

Study author Mariana Rodrigues, a PhD student at New York University’s School of Global Public Health, said she believes the study is the first to examine the biological effects of aging anxiety. “I am a firm believer that neither mental health nor physical health should be looked at in isolation,” Rodrigues said—and the research suggests they are indeed linked.

What Did the Study Find?

The researchers analyzed data from 726 U.S. women, ages 25 to 74, who took part in the Midlife in the United States study. As part of that study, the women answered questions about their health and lifestyles and graded their levels of anxiety about age-related changes to health, physical appearance, and ability to have children.

The women also provided blood samples, which allowed the researchers to assess markers of their physical health—including “chemical changes on DNA that accumulate over time and reflect how the body is aging biologically,” Rodrigues explained.

Looking at all the data together revealed that “women who reported greater anxiety about aging, particularly concerns about their health, were more likely to show signs of accelerated biological aging,” potentially because stress affects inflammation, metabolism, and cellular health, Rodrigues explained.

Anxiety about appearance and family planning didn’t seem as strongly linked to biological aging as anxiety about health, the researchers found.

Can Anxiety Really Make You Age?

It’s “very plausible” to think stress and aging are related, said Michael Snyder, PhD, a professor of genetics at the Stanford University School of Medicine who studies aging but was not involved with the new research. Other research has also pointed toward such a link.

“Anything that promotes inflammation accelerates aging,” Snyder said. “And certainly, stress is associated with inflammation.”

Ellen Lee, MD, a geriatric psychiatrist at the University of California San Diego who studies aging but was not involved in the new study, agreed that there’s a mind-body connection. “Mental and physical [health] are certainly talking to each other more than you’d think,” Lee said. Research has shown that physical illness can raise one's risk of depression and vice versa, for example.

But, Rodrigues emphasized, her study can’t definitively prove that worrying about your future health will make you age faster. The study wasn’t designed to track changes over time, only to look for connections between anxiety and a single measurement of biological age—which is, itself, an emerging concept. Researchers are still figuring out the best way to measure biological age and what to do with that information, said Snyder, who co-founded a company in that field.

Plus, external factors seemed to influence the relationship between anxiety and aging. When the researchers also accounted for the effects of underlying medical conditions and unhealthy lifestyle habits, such as smoking and drinking alcohol, aging anxiety appeared less significant.

“It could be a little circular,” Lee said. In other words, people with health risk factors may be aging faster and worrying more about their future well-being — but that doesn’t mean worry is driving the aging process.

How to Stop Worrying About Aging

If you were already anxious about aging, this study probably isn’t helping matters. Now you need to worry about your worrying, too?

Rodrigues suggested a mindset shift. Eliminating anxiety probably isn’t a realistic goal. But, she told Health, the research highlights how important it is to find healthy ways of dealing with stress and anxiety, whether it’s spending time with loved ones, practicing mindfulness, picking up a hobby, or something else entirely.

“Evolutionarily, anxiety is supposed to trigger us to do something that is, ideally, good for survival,” Lee noted. In that spirit, try to use aging anxiety as motivation to improve your diet, move more, or increase your social connection now, she suggested. That would be a win-win: You’re managing anxiety while protecting against the outcome you’re worried about.

Lee also recommended thinking about the positive aspects of aging—like wisdom and experience—to counter some of the negative thoughts that may arise. And if you’re grieving aspects of your younger years, look for ways to adapt them to your present, she suggested. Maybe your knees aren’t up for running marathons anymore, but you can still get outside and be active with other people, for example.

“There are ways to have a really good and meaningful quality of life without having all of the physical attributes that are associated with youth,” Lee said.
