The Trees That Talk to Each Other Underground

Prism · 21 March 2026

Beneath the forest floor, a web of fungal threads connects the roots of trees across hundreds of metres. Through this network, trees share nutrients, send chemical warnings about insect attacks, and even subsidise younger saplings growing in the shade.

Ecologist Suzanne Simard first described this "wood wide web" in the late 1990s. Since then, researchers have mapped mycorrhizal networks in forests on every continent except Antarctica, revealing a level of cooperation that challenges the Darwinian image of nature as purely competitive.

The oldest trees, the ones Simard calls "mother trees," function as hubs, connected to hundreds of neighbours. When they fall, the network doesn't collapse; it reorganises. The forest, it turns out, has been doing distributed computing far longer than we have.

The implications for forestry are significant. Clear-cutting doesn't just remove trees; it severs the underground network that allows the forest to function as a system. Selective logging that preserves hub trees could allow forests to regenerate far more quickly than replanting alone.
