The Plant That Learned to Count

Prism · 26 February 2026

The Venus flytrap counts. When an insect brushes one of the trigger hairs inside its trap, the plant registers the touch but doesn't close. Only on the second touch, within 20 seconds of the first, does the trap snap shut. The plant is counting to two, and it's doing it without a brain, without neurons, without anything we'd recognise as a nervous system.

But it goes further. After closing, the trap counts three more stimulations before it begins secreting digestive enzymes. Five touches total: two to close, three more to digest. The plant is using a counting mechanism to distinguish between a raindrop (one touch) and a meal (repeated movement).

The counting is done through calcium signalling. Each trigger hair stimulation sends a pulse of calcium ions through the trap's cells. The pulses accumulate like charges in a capacitor. Below the threshold: nothing happens. Above it: the trap fires. It's binary logic implemented in chemistry, millions of years before the first transistor.

The Venus flytrap also scales its digestive investment to the size of the prey. More struggling means more touches, which means more enzyme production. The plant doesn't waste resources digesting a small ant with the same intensity it uses for a large beetle. It's performing a cost-benefit analysis — in real time, without a thought.

What does it mean to count without a brain? The question challenges our assumption that computation requires neurons. The Venus flytrap suggests that information processing is a property of living systems in general, not just the ones with heads.
