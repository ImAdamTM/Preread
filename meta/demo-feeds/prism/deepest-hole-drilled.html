The Deepest Hole Ever Drilled (and Why We Stopped)

Prism · 17 March 2026

In 1970, Soviet scientists began drilling into the Kola Peninsula with a simple goal: go as deep as possible. Over the next 24 years, they reached 12,262 metres — a record that still stands. Then they stopped, not because they ran out of ambition, but because the Earth fought back.

At that depth, temperatures hit 180 degrees Celsius, far hotter than models predicted. The rock behaved less like a solid and more like a plastic, closing the borehole behind the drill bit. The project revealed that our understanding of what lies beneath the crust was, in many ways, guesswork.

What they found

Among the surprises: liquid water was found at depths where none was expected to exist. The prevailing theory held that rock at those pressures would be too dense and compressed to contain water. Instead, the drill bit hit mineralised water that had been trapped in the rock for billions of years, sealed under pressures that should have squeezed it out of existence.

Microscopic fossils of plankton appeared in rock two billion years old — far deeper than any biological material had been found before. The boundary between granite and basalt, which seismic studies had predicted at around five kilometres, never materialised. What geologists had interpreted as a sharp geological boundary turned out to be a gradual transition caused by metamorphic changes in the granite itself.

In other words, the textbook model of the crust — neat layers of rock stacked like a cake — was wrong. The reality was messier, wetter, and more alive than anyone had imagined.

Why we stopped

The project wasn't cancelled for lack of funding, though the collapse of the Soviet Union didn't help. The fundamental problem was physics. At 12 kilometres, the drill string — over 12 kilometres of steel pipe — weighed so much that it was approaching the tensile strength of the material itself. The pipe wanted to snap under its own weight before the bit could cut rock.

The heat was the other killer. Drilling lubricants broke down. Electronic instruments failed. The rock at that depth was so hot and plastic that the borehole would begin closing as soon as the drill bit was withdrawn, like pushing a straw into warm butter and watching the hole seal behind it.

The site was finally closed in 2005. The borehole was capped with a rusted metal lid bolted to the bedrock. Today, it sits in an abandoned complex on the Kola Peninsula, surrounded by ruined buildings and tundra. The deepest point humans have ever reached on their own planet is marked by a welded steel cap roughly the size of a manhole cover.

The frontier beneath

We've sent probes to the edge of the solar system, landed robots on Mars, and photographed a black hole 55 million light-years away. But the deepest we've ever gone into our own planet is about 0.2 percent of the way to the centre. The entire history of deep drilling has barely scratched the outermost shell.

New projects are attempting to revive the effort. Japan's Chikyu research vessel is designed to drill through the ocean floor, where the crust is thinner, hoping to reach the mantle for the first time. If it succeeds, it will access rock that has never been exposed to the surface — material from the engine room of plate tectonics.

The Earth keeps most of its secrets close. But we haven't stopped asking.
