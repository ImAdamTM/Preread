The Fungus That Eats Radiation for Breakfast

Prism · 8 March 2026

Inside the ruined reactor at Chernobyl, a black fungus grows toward the radiation source the way a plant grows toward light. Cladosporium sphaerospermum doesn't just tolerate ionising radiation — it appears to use it as an energy source, converting gamma rays into chemical energy through a process called radiosynthesis.

The melanin in its cell walls absorbs radiation and drives metabolic reactions, much the way chlorophyll absorbs sunlight. It's photosynthesis's dark twin, powered not by a star but by a melted nuclear core.

NASA is now studying the fungus as a potential radiation shield for spacecraft. A thin layer of living fungus could, in theory, protect astronauts on long-duration missions — a biological solution to a problem we've been trying to solve with lead and concrete.

Life, it turns out, doesn't just survive in extreme environments. It finds ways to make those environments useful. Evolution doesn't care about our categories of habitable and uninhabitable. It just keeps experimenting.
