The Bookshop at the End of the World

Meridian · 27 February 2026

Wigtown is a town of 900 people on the southwest coast of Scotland. It has one pub, one post office, and eleven bookshops. The entire town is a bookshop, or rather, a town that decided to become one.

In 1998, Wigtown was dying. The local economy had collapsed, young people were leaving, and the high street was empty. Then someone proposed an experiment: designate the town as Scotland's National Book Town and invite second-hand booksellers to set up shop in the abandoned buildings.

It worked. The bookshops drew visitors, the visitors drew cafés, and the cafés drew residents. Today, 100,000 people a year visit a town that most maps barely acknowledge.

The largest bookshop, The Book Shop, has over a mile of shelving and a resident cat named Captain who sleeps on the biography section. The smallest, a shed behind the post office, specialises in Scottish poetry and is open by appointment only. Between them, the town holds an estimated quarter of a million books.

The annual book festival, held every September, draws authors from across the world to a marquee in the town gardens. For one week, a place with no traffic lights and one cash machine becomes a literary capital. Then the marquee comes down, the visitors leave, and Wigtown goes back to being quietly, determinedly itself.

The lesson isn't about books — it's about identity. Give a place a reason to exist, and people will find a reason to come.
