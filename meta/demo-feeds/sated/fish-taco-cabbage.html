The Fish Taco That Changed My Mind About Cabbage

Sated · 14 March 2026

I spent most of my twenties ignoring cabbage entirely. It showed up in sad coleslaws and forgettable stir-fries, and I never gave it a second thought. Then I ate a fish taco in Ensenada that ruined every fish taco I'd had before it.

The secret was the cabbage. Shredded paper-thin, tossed with lime juice, a little salt, and left to sit for exactly ten minutes. Not five, not twenty. Ten. Enough time for the acid to soften the fibres without turning them limp.

It sat on top of battered white fish, underneath a drizzle of chipotle crema, and it was the best thing on the plate. The crunch cut through the richness of the batter. The lime kept everything bright. The cabbage wasn't a garnish — it was the structural element that held the whole taco together.

The taco stand that started it

The stand didn't have a name. It was a metal cart on a street corner in Ensenada's fish market, run by a woman in her sixties and her teenage grandson. She battered the fish — corvina, caught that morning — in a thin, crispy coating that shattered when you bit into it. The grandson assembled each taco with the seriousness of a surgeon: tortilla, fish, cabbage, crema, a single slice of avocado, and a squeeze of lime from a cut half he kept in his apron pocket.

I ordered three. Then two more. Then stood there eating a sixth while the grandson pretended not to notice. At roughly a dollar fifty each, it was the best meal I ate in a month that included two restaurants with tablecloths.

Why the cabbage works

Most taco recipes call for iceberg lettuce. This is wrong. Iceberg has water and crunch but no flavour. Green cabbage, shredded thin, has a peppery bite that stands up to fried fish the way lettuce never can. It also has structural integrity — it won't wilt and turn your tortilla into a soggy envelope five minutes after assembly.

The lime juice does double duty. It softens the raw edge of the cabbage and creates a bright acidic counterpoint to the richness of the batter and the fat of the crema. Without it, the taco is flat. With it, every bite has tension — crunch against soft, acid against fat, cool against warm.

The ten-minute rest is non-negotiable. Less than that and the cabbage is too raw, too aggressive. More and it starts to lose its snap. The window is narrow, but hitting it is the difference between a good taco and a transcendent one.

Bringing it home

I've made these at home fifty times since that trip. The fish doesn't need to be corvina — any firm white fish works. Cod, halibut, even pollock. The batter is just flour, cornstarch, salt, and sparkling water, mixed until barely combined. Lumps are fine. Lumps are good. Overmixing develops gluten, and gluten makes batter chewy instead of crispy.

Fry at 190 degrees until golden. Drain on a wire rack, not paper towels — paper towels trap steam and soften the bottom. Assemble immediately.

Sometimes the supporting actor steals the whole show.
