Why Brutalism Looks Better With Age

Atelier · 12 March 2026

Brutalist buildings were controversial when they were new. Half a century later, the ones that survived demolition campaigns are among the most photographed structures in their cities. The Barbican in London. Habitat 67 in Montreal. The National Theatre on the South Bank.

Concrete ages in a way that glass and steel don't. It stains, weathers, and accumulates character. Moss fills the board-formed grain. Rain streaks create patterns that the architect never drew. The building becomes a collaboration between the designer and time itself.

The problem with first impressions

Brutalism's original sin was timing. The style emerged in the post-war period when speed and economy mattered more than charm. Many Brutalist buildings were social housing, schools, and civic centres — public infrastructure built on tight budgets for communities that had no say in the design. The architecture was imposed, not chosen, and the resentment was understandable.

It didn't help that concrete in its first decade looks clinical. The formwork marks are sharp, the colour is uniform grey, and the scale can be overwhelming. Glass-and-steel buildings photograph well from day one. Concrete buildings need time to earn their beauty, and most critics don't wait that long.

By the 1980s, demolition had become the default response. Councils across Britain knocked down housing estates that were structurally sound but aesthetically unpopular. In the process, they destroyed buildings that, had they survived another twenty years, would have aged into the kind of textured, monumental presence that people now travel to photograph.

What survived

The buildings that made it through the demolition era tend to be the ones with the strongest advocates — usually the people who actually lived or worked in them. The Barbican's residents fought multiple proposals to redevelop the estate and won every time. Today, flats there sell for millions, and the waiting list for the housing association units stretches into years.

The National Theatre was almost demolished in the 1990s. Prince Charles famously called it "a clever way of building a nuclear power station in the middle of London." Two decades later, it's a Grade II listed building and the most visited theatre in the world. The concrete that Charles objected to is now streaked with decades of London weather, softened by moss and lichen, and beautiful in a way that no rendering could have predicted.

The sustainability argument

The current generation of architects citing Brutalist influences aren't nostalgic; they're practical. Exposed concrete is honest, durable, and requires almost no maintenance. A concrete building doesn't need repainting every five years, re-cladding every twenty, or re-sealing every decade. Its maintenance cost over a century approaches zero.

There's a carbon cost to producing concrete, and it's significant. But when measured over a building's full lifespan — not just its construction — the calculation shifts. A building that lasts 200 years with no maintenance has a lower annualised carbon footprint than a building that lasts 40 years and needs three major refurbishments.

Perhaps the lesson is that beauty in architecture, as in people, is sometimes a function of time rather than intention. The buildings we love most are rarely the ones that looked best on their first day. They're the ones that stuck around long enough to prove their worth.
