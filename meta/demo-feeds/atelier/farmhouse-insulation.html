What a 400-Year-Old Farmhouse Knows About Insulation

Atelier · 3 March 2026

The stone walls of a seventeenth-century Cotswold farmhouse are 600 millimetres thick. They have no insulation, no cavity, and no vapour barrier. They also keep the house cool in summer and warm in winter, performing better than many modern builds with a fraction of the technology.

The principle is thermal mass. Dense stone absorbs heat slowly during the day and releases it slowly at night, smoothing out temperature swings that would send a timber-frame house reaching for the thermostat. The walls are both structure and climate system, doing two jobs with one material.

Modern retrofitters often make the mistake of wrapping old stone buildings in external insulation, trapping moisture inside walls that were designed to breathe. The result is damp, mould, and a building that performs worse than it did before the "improvement."

Modern building science is rediscovering what vernacular builders knew instinctively: sometimes the best insulation strategy isn't to trap heat — it's to slow it down.
