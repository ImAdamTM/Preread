The Case for Logging Off at Five

Currentwave · 15 March 2026

Remote work promised freedom. For many, it delivered a longer workday with blurrier boundaries. The laptop is always within arm's reach, and Slack never truly sleeps.

A growing body of research suggests that hard stops aren't just good for wellbeing — they're good for output. Microsoft's own internal studies found that employees who disconnected fully at the end of the day produced measurably better work the following morning than those who kept half an eye on their inbox through the evening.

The mechanism is straightforward. Cognitive resources deplete over the course of a day and replenish during genuine rest. "Genuine" is the key word. Scrolling through work messages on the sofa isn't rest; it's low-grade vigilance that prevents the brain from entering the recovery states it needs.

Setting a firm boundary isn't laziness. It's maintenance. The best tools are useless when the person operating them is running on fumes.
