The Rise of the 32-Hour Work Week

Currentwave · 7 March 2026

Iceland ran the world's largest trial of a shorter work week between 2015 and 2019. The results were unequivocal: productivity held steady or improved, employee wellbeing rose dramatically, and burnout plummeted. Now dozens of companies worldwide are following suit.

The 32-hour week doesn't mean cramming five days into four. It means rethinking which meetings, reports, and processes actually produce value — and cutting the ones that don't. Most organisations, when forced to be honest, discover that a startling amount of their work week is performative.

The companies that have adopted shorter weeks report the same pattern: a brief adjustment period of mild panic, followed by a permanent improvement in focus. When you have less time, you waste less of it. The two-hour meeting becomes a twenty-minute stand-up. The weekly status report becomes a shared document that updates itself.

Critics argue it only works for knowledge workers, and there's truth to that — you can't run a hospital ward in 32 hours. But for the significant chunk of the economy that operates on laptops and conference calls, the question isn't whether shorter weeks work. It's why we're still pretending they don't.
